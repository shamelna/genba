# Module 1 — Leading Self: Complete Content Guide

*Genba Ikigai Leadership Development App*

---

## Overview

Module 1 is the foundation of the Genba Ikigai journey. It consists of two sequential case study chapters, four core leadership habits, a 18-practice micro-practice system, and a habit-insight journaling framework — all centred on the single premise that transformation in a team can only begin with honest transformation in the leader.

The module uses two characters throughout: **Sarah Chen**, a production manager eight years into her role, and **Kenji Tanaka**, an external Genba coach with 31 years of lean leadership experience. Their story unfolds across five weeks.

---

## Part 1 — The Introduction Case Study: *The Diagnosis*

### Premise

Sarah is a highly capable, experienced production manager who has built a team entirely dependent on her presence. Kenji arrives as an external coach, declines her data pack, and asks simply to walk the floor. In one morning, he sees what eight years of proximity has made invisible to Sarah.

### Act-by-Act Summary

**Act 1 — The Arrival.** Kenji declines information and requests only a walk. Sarah is thrown. She is not used to someone declining data. The walk begins.

**Act 2 — The Walk.** Kenji observes silently while Sarah narrates. He notices: a visual board with fallen sticky notes and no apparent owner; a quality problem being solved by Marek alone, with no visible methodology; two idle operators who stop work and wait for Sarah to answer a question they could resolve themselves. He says almost nothing. He simply sees.

**Act 3 — The Office.** Back in her office, Sarah offers a data pack. Kenji declines and asks one question: *"When you are not here, Sarah — what happens?"* Sarah answers confidently. Kenji clarifies — he did not ask what the team *does*. He asked what *happens*. Sarah finds she has no honest answer.

**Act 4 — The Question.** Kenji names the three observations plainly: operators have learned to wait for Sarah; Marek has learned to contain problems rather than surface them; the visual board speaks Sarah's language, not the team's. He ends with one question for Sarah to hold — not answer — before Thursday: *"Why does your team need you to be present for things to work?"*

### End-of-Chapter Reflection Questions

1. When you are not present — what actually happens in your team or area of responsibility? Not what you think happens. What actually happens.
2. In what ways might your team have learned to depend on you rather than develop themselves? What did you do — even with good intentions — that taught them this?
3. What is one habit of yours that may be preventing the people around you from growing?

### Workbook Prompts (Stop and Think)

The Introduction workbook is structured in four parts:

**Part 1 — Your Ikigai:** What aspects of leadership genuinely energise you? What are you naturally good at? What does your team or organisation most need right now? What does good leadership look like in your context?

**Part 2 — Leading to Serve:** Think of a leader who had a lasting positive impact on you. When have you felt most useful as a leader — not most in control, but most useful? What would it mean to lead in service of your team's growth?

**Part 3 — The 16 Habits:** Which of the 16 habits of a Genba leader feels most natural right now? Which feels most challenging? What is one small daily action you could take this week?

**Part 4 — Two Paths:** Path A — you continue leading as you lead now. What does your team look like in 12 months? Path B — you commit to one significant change. What changes, and what does that cost you? Which path is more honest? Which is more necessary?

### Journal Prompt

*"Describe a recent situation where you solved a problem your team could have solved itself. What stopped you from stepping back? What did it cost them — in capability, in confidence, in trust? Make a list of open questions you could ask the team. The key point is: take a look at yourself as in a mirror and ask, what could you have done differently so that your team becomes more self-reliant in problem solving in the future?"*

## Part 2 — The Leading Self Case Study: *Four Weeks. Four Habits. One Transformation.*

### Premise

This is the main content of Module 1. Picking up directly after the Introduction, it follows Sarah across four weeks — one week per habit — as she works to dismantle a leadership style built on being indispensable, and replaces it with one built on developing others.

---

### Act 1 — The Mirror *(Hansei: Self-Reflection)*

**Summary:** That evening after Kenji's first visit, Sarah writes in her notebook. She traces how a team that was struggling eight years ago became a team that depends entirely on her presence. She names the moment it shifted from building a team to replacing them. She sees it clearly — and cannot unsee it.

**Key moment:** Sarah's journal entry captures the insight: *"It started well. I worked hard. I learned everything. I solved the problems. And it worked. The numbers improved. People noticed. I got promoted. But somewhere along the way, I stopped developing people. I started replacing them."*

**Habit taught:** Hansei is defined here not as guilt or shame but as *clear seeing* — honest self-reflection and the commitment to do better.

**Per-act reflection questions (shown between Acts 1 and 2):**
1. What did you notice about Sarah in this act? Describe what stood out.
2. What did Kenji see that she didn't see? What was his insight?
3. Where do you see this pattern — not looking, avoiding — in your own leadership?

---

### Act 2 — Stepping Back *(Humility: Stepping Back)*

**Summary:** On Thursday, Kenji returns. Sarah tells him she knows why her team needs her — because she made them need her. Kenji names her fears directly: she is afraid things will fail, afraid of being less necessary, afraid of her team's blame. He gives her one assignment: for three days, she will not solve any problems.

**Day One:** A quality issue. Sarah's reflex is to go and fix it. She stops herself. She asks: *"If I were not here, how would you solve this?"* The operator blinks. She walks away. One hour later, the line is running. The operator and Marek solved it together. It took longer. It happened.

**Day Two:** A scheduling conflict. Sarah asks the two affected workers to decide between themselves. They have a conversation they have not needed to have in years. They solve it. They walk taller.

**Day Three:** By afternoon, people stop coming to find Sarah. They start solving things first — then come to her with a proposal rather than a question.

**The Debrief:** Kenji names what happened: humility is not weakness or saying yes to everything. It is stepping back so others can step forward. His closing assignment: have a conversation with each person on the floor asking *"What opportunity have I been taking from you by solving everything myself?"*

**Habit taught:** Humility as the practice of the inverted pyramid — the leader at the bottom, supporting from below, not the hero at the top.

**Per-act reflection questions (shown between Acts 2 and 3):**
1. What did you feel watching Sarah step back and let her team try?
2. Where do you try to control things your team could solve?
3. What would change if you trusted them more?

---

### Act 3 — Real Commitments *(Integrity: Keeping Commitments)*

**Summary:** Sarah has five conversations — one with each of her leaders — asking what opportunity she has been taking from them. Each reveals something she has been carrying that belonged to them.

**Marek** (quality): Sarah tells him directly that she did not trust anyone to do it her way, so she took the role from him. She offers him full ownership of the quality process. Eight years of waiting is named out loud.

**Elena** (training): Sarah admits she made a commitment to Elena before — that she would have the training role — and broke it repeatedly. She gives it back. When Elena asks *"Even if I make mistakes?"* Sarah answers: *"Especially if you make mistakes. That is how you learn."*

**Thomas, Yuki, Ahmed:** Similar conversations about scheduling, inventory, and team representation. Each one reveals what Sarah has been taking. Each one receives a real commitment and a genuine acknowledgment of capability.

**Kenji's visit:** Kenji challenges Sarah's version of the commitments. She has given people roles, but she is still the final approval on everything — still the safety net. That is not ownership; it is permission to try while she watches. *"Make fewer promises and keep them all."*

**Habit taught:** Integrity as the alignment between words and actions — not perfect promises, but real ones. Trust is built in small, repeated follow-through, not in big gestures.

**Per-act reflection questions (shown between Acts 3 and 4):**
1. Which of Sarah's conversations hit home for you? Why?
2. What commitment have YOU made to your team that you're not currently keeping?
3. What would it look like to keep that commitment for 30 days?

---

### Act 4 — Seeing People *(Respect & Mutual Trust: Seeing People)*

**Summary:** Kenji's final assignment — have a respect conversation. Tell someone what you see in them. Tell them you believe in their capability. Sarah spends days trying to write what she wants to say and failing. By Friday afternoon, she has nothing written — only a feeling: *I need to say something true.*

**Conversation with Marek:** Sarah tells him she watched him step into the quality role and teach people — not just fix things. She names clearly: *"For eight years I have been preventing you from doing that. Not because I did not see your capability. Because I was too afraid to let anyone else be good at what I thought was my job."* Marek's eyes water. *"I have waited eight years for that."*

**The other conversations:** Variations of this with Elena, Thomas, Yuki, and Ahmed. Some cry. Some are skeptical — they have learned not to trust Sarah's words. But something shifts in each conversation. Something unspoken is finally said out loud.

**Kenji's final visit:** He synthesises the four habits by tracing them back through Sarah's weeks. She has practiced all four — not perfectly, but really. He tells her he will not come back on Thursdays. She does not need him to. She needs to practice. He will return on Week 8, not to teach her, but to see what she has built.

**Kenji's closing words:** *"You asked me at the beginning: what do you want me to do? And I said: nothing. Just look honestly at what you have built. You have done that. You have looked. And now you are building something different. That is everything."*

**Sarah's final journal entry:** She writes all four habits back in her own words — Hansei as honest self-seeing, Humility as stepping back, Integrity as keeping the promise every time, Respect as telling people what she sees in them and meaning it. She ends: *"I am not there yet. But I am trying. Day by day. Commitment by commitment. And maybe that is enough."*

**Habit taught:** Respect as the practice of genuinely seeing people — their capability, their potential, their waiting — and naming it out loud.

**Per-act reflection questions (shown between Acts 3 and 4):**
1. Who on your team do you need to SEE better?
2. What capability or potential do they have that you haven't acknowledged?
3. How will you show them that you see them?

---

### End-of-Module Reflection Questions

1. Which of the four habits does Sarah struggle with most — Hansei, Humility, Integrity, or Respect? What makes that one the hardest for her? And which one is hardest for YOU?
2. In Week 1, Sarah could not answer Kenji's question: "Why does your team need you?" What would YOUR team say if someone asked the same question about you?
3. In Week 3, Sarah makes a distinction: "I promised my team these things before, but I broke those promises." What commitments have YOU made to your team that you are struggling to keep? Why?
4. In Week 4, Sarah tells Marek: "I have been preventing you from doing that. Not because I did not see your capability. Because I was too afraid." What is ONE thing you are afraid to let your team do?
5. What is the difference between Sarah's "strong leadership" at the beginning and her "strong leadership" at the end? Which version is actually stronger?

### Journal Prompt

*"Imagine it is Week 1 for you. Someone asks you the same question Kenji asked Sarah: 'When you are not present, what actually happens in your area?' Write your honest answer — not the answer you would give in an interview. The real answer. What does your team actually do? What have you taught them to depend on you for? Then ask yourself: what is one small commitment you can make this week that you can actually keep?"*

## Part 3 — The Four Core Habits

These four habits are the structural backbone of Module 1. Each maps to one act of the Leading Self story and drives the journaling, micro-practice, and reflection systems.

### 🪞 Hansei (Self-Reflection)

The practice of honest self-seeing. Not guilt. Not shame. Looking clearly at what is true, acknowledging it, and committing to do better. In Sarah's story, Hansei is the moment she stops defending and starts seeing — the night after Kenji's first visit when she traces how the dependency she created came to be.

**Habit Insight journal prompts:**
- What did you notice about yourself as you learned this habit? (How did honest self-seeing feel?)
- Where in your leadership do you see the impact of NOT doing Hansei? (What are you avoiding looking at?)
- One moment this week where you'll practice Hansei. (Pick a specific time/situation.)
- *Optional:* What surprised you most about Sarah's Hansei journey?

### 🙏 Humility & Gratitude (Stepping Back)

The practice of creating space for others by removing yourself from the centre. Not weakness — inverted leadership. The leader at the bottom of the pyramid, supporting from below. In Sarah's story, this is the three days of not solving anything and discovering her team is capable.

**Habit Insight journal prompts:**
- What felt true about stepping back and letting others lead? (What scared you? What excited you?)
- Where do you currently solve things your team could solve? (What do you take from them by doing it?)
- One decision this week you'll ask about instead of solving.
- *Optional:* Who on your team showed you humility this week?

### 🤝 Integrity (Keeping Commitments)

The practice of aligning words and actions — not perfect promises, but real ones kept over time. Trust is built in small, repeated follow-through, not in moments of good intention. In Sarah's story, this is the five conversations where she makes genuine commitments, admits to broken ones, and asks to be held accountable.

**Habit Insight journal prompts:**
- What commitment have you made to your team that you're NOT currently keeping? (Be specific.)
- What's stopping you from keeping it? (What's the real barrier?)
- One commitment you'll focus on for the next 30 days.
- *Optional:* Who shows you integrity in how they lead?

### 👁️ Respect & Mutual Trust (Seeing People)

The practice of genuinely seeing people — their capability, their potential, what they are waiting to be trusted with — and naming it out loud. Not as a gesture or technique, but as a real acknowledgment. In Sarah's story, this is telling Marek *"I see you"* after eight years of not saying it.

**Habit Insight journal prompts:**
- Who on your team do you need to see more clearly? (Describe them in 2–3 sentences.)
- What capability do you see in them that you haven't fully acknowledged?
- One way you'll show them you see them. (Tell them, give them authority, ask their opinion.)
- *Optional:* Who made you feel truly seen by their leadership?

## Part 4 — The Daily Micro-Practice System

### Structure

18 self-paced practices across 2 cycles. No locked days or penalties. Learners advance to the next practice when they mark the current one complete. Practices rotate through the four habits with one integration practice at the end of each cycle.

Each practice card shows: the habit name and colour, a single focused prompt, brief guidance, estimated time, and the option to add a journal note or skip for today.

## Part 5 — The 16 Habits of a Genba Leader

These are the broader habit library surfaced in the Introduction workbook. They represent the full map of Genba leadership behaviours from which the Module 1 habits are drawn.

1. **Humility** — Leading with service; thinking of yourself less, not thinking less of yourself
2. **Servant Leadership (Inverted Pyramid)** — Supporting those who do the work; customers at the top, leaders at the bottom
3. **Genchi Genbutsu (Go and See)** — Going to the actual place of work; decisions based on facts, not assumptions
4. **Hansei (Self-Reflection)** — Continuous improvement through honest reflection; acknowledging mistakes and learning
5. **Standard Work** — Documenting the best known way to perform work; a baseline for improvement
6. **Mendoumi (Member Care)** — Taking care of team members; building relationships based on trust and respect
7. **Daily Management Practice (DMP)** — Managing through daily visual controls and leader standard work
8. **Visual Management** — Making work and performance visible; a self-explaining workplace
9. **Problem Solving (PDCA)** — Plan-Do-Check-Act methodology; scientific thinking over jumping to solutions
10. **Hoshin Kanri (Strategy Deployment)** — Aligning the organisation through strategic planning; everyone working toward the same goals
11. **Kaizen (Continuous Improvement)** — Small, incremental daily improvements; the compound effect of daily change
12. **TWI (Training Within Industry)** — Structured training methods; consistent knowledge transfer
13. **PDCA — Climbing the Mountain** — Applying systematic thinking to breakthrough goals
14. **Engaging Others** — Involving team members in improvement and problem solving; creating ownership
15. **Andon (Supporting Others)** — Systems that signal problems immediately; a culture where asking for help is encouraged
16. **Yokoten (Sharing Best Practices)** — Spreading successful improvements; learning from others and adapting solutions

## How the Systems Connect

The four elements of Module 1 are designed to reinforce each other:

The **case study** (Introduction + Leading Self) gives learners a mirror — they watch Sarah go through the transformation before being asked to go through it themselves. Each act of the story is immediately followed by reflection prompts that pivot from Sarah's experience to the learner's own.

The **Habit Insight journal** is the deep reflection layer. After reading an act or completing practices, learners write against structured prompts that push past surface response toward specific, honest insight. Each habit has 3 required prompts and 1 optional one.

The **Daily Micro-Practice system** is the behavioural layer. Short, specific prompts (2–15 minutes) give learners a daily practice anchor. The system is self-paced and non-punitive — no locked days, no streaks required — because the content explicitly teaches that genuine change is built through real commitment, not compliance.

The **16 Habits reference library** sits beneath all of this as the full framework from which Module 1 habits are drawn. Learners are introduced to the full map in the Introduction workbook before the narrative narrows focus to the four habits of Leading Self.
